*Building Costs and Labor Shortages: How Tariffs and Immigration Policies Could Reshape Housing Affordability*

The housing market faces mounting pressures from proposed economic policies. Two significant issues — tariffs on building materials and potential mass deportations of undocumented workers — could substantially increase construction costs and reduce housing affordability, particularly impacting middle-class families.

## Tariffs on Building Materials

![Where our building materials come from](/assets/images/blog/proposed-policies-building-materials-sources.png): What They Mean for Housing Costs

### Where Do Our Building Materials Come From?

**Lumber (Wood):**

- The U.S. obtains approximately 28% of softwood lumber imports from Canada, essential for home framing and structural components
- Previous tariffs on Canadian lumber raised new home costs by an estimated $9,000 per unit
- Proposed 25% tariffs on Canadian imports could produce similar or greater increases

**Steel and Aluminum:**

- Canada, Mexico, and China supply significant quantities used in framing, roofing, and infrastructure
- A proposed 60% tariff on Chinese imports and 25% on Mexican and Canadian shipments could substantially elevate costs
- Roofing materials, appliances, and HVAC systems would become considerably more expensive

**Manufactured Goods:**

![Tariff impact on construction materials](/assets/images/blog/proposed-policies-tariff-impact.png)

- Drywall, flooring, and light fixtures frequently originate from China
- Heavy tariff burdens on Chinese products would cascade through construction, inflating costs at every stage

### Implications for Housing Costs

![Housing cost implications](/assets/images/blog/proposed-policies-housing-costs.png)

- **Higher Material Costs:** Builders would absorb elevated expenses, passing them directly to homebuyers
- **Delays in Housing Supply:** Rising costs may discourage new construction projects, further constraining already limited housing inventory
- **Middle-Class Impact:** With affordability already in crisis, price increases would disproportionately exclude middle-income purchasers from the market

## Deporting Immigrants: A Labor Crisis in the Making

### The Role of Immigrants in the Construction Industry

- Immigrants comprise 24% of the U.S. construction workforce, with 13% undocumented
- They predominantly perform labor-intensive tasks including framing, drywall installation, roofing, and concrete work
- These positions remain difficult to fill with domestic workers due to physical demands and relatively modest compensation

### Implications of Mass Deportations

![Construction labor shortage statistics](/assets/images/blog/proposed-policies-labor-shortage.png)

**Labor Shortages:** The construction sector already faces severe workforce deficits, with over 400,000 unfilled positions as of 2023. Removing millions of undocumented workers would intensify this shortage dramatically.

**Higher Labor Costs:** Contractors would need to raise wages to attract domestic workers. Though beneficial for U.S. workers individually, this translates into significantly elevated homebuyer expenses.

**Reduced Housing Starts:** Fewer available workers combined with higher expenses would prompt builders to scale back projects, exacerbating the housing supply crisis.

**Disaster Recovery Delays:** Immigrant labor has proven critical for post-disaster reconstruction. Mass deportations could substantially extend recovery periods following hurricanes, wildfires, and similar events.

## Housing Starts: A Key to Housing Affordability

Housing starts have declined substantially in recent years. The U.S. Census Bureau reported a 13.7% year-over-year decrease in 2022, reflecting labor shortages, escalating material costs, and economic uncertainty. This decline further tightens housing supply, making affordability increasingly difficult for middle-class families.

### How Proposed Policies Could Impact Housing Starts

**Tariffs on Building Materials:** Higher material costs discourage new development initiatives. Smaller builders serving local communities with affordable housing would bear disproportionate impacts, potentially ceasing operations or reducing activities.

**Deportation of Immigrant Workers:** Significant labor gaps would elevate costs and delay projects. Affordable housing initiatives, operating on thin margins with cost-effective labor dependence, would suffer particularly severe consequences.

**The Affordability Problem:** Reduced housing starts mean even tighter supply in an undersupplied market. The National Association of Realtors estimates a shortage of 6.5 million homes. These policies could substantially worsen this deficit, continuing price increases that disproportionately affect middle- and lower-income families.

## A Perfect Storm for the Middle Class

Combined, these policies create concerning consequences:

1. **Rising Prices:** Elevated material and labor costs increase new home prices, pushing homeownership further beyond reach for many families

2. **Slower Market Activity:** Reduced housing starts and extended construction timelines tighten inventory, driving up existing home prices simultaneously

3. **Economic Ripple Effects:** Declining construction activity could impact related industries — appliances, furniture — reducing overall economic growth and employment

## What Can We Do?

Understanding these policies' complexities proves essential. While tariffs and immigration reforms may appear straightforward protective measures, the reality involves significant trade-offs. Policies increasing costs and reducing workforce ultimately harm the middle-class families they intend supporting.
